In a desert landscape that stretches from the Valley of the Sun to the rugged Superstition Mountains, the trails near Mesa, Arizona offer some of the most dramatic hiking terrain in the American Southwest. Seasoned adventurers and casual walkers alike find routes to match their ambition.
Our correspondents have spent the spring season traversing these paths, documenting elevation profiles, marking trailheads, and collecting testimony from the hikers who know these routes best. The results are presented herein for your edification.
From the 860-foot ascent to Wind Cave at Usery Mountain to the punishing 2,045-foot climb of Siphon Draw, these trails test every hiker differently. We present five routes ranked by difficulty, with full data and independent traveler reviews.

Trail Reports

Trail No. I
Wind Cave Trail Rewards Moderate Hikers with Panoramic Views
By Our Outdoor Correspondent · Usery Mountain Regional Park

The wind cave formation, carved by centuries of erosion into the volcanic tuff of Usery Mountain, offers a dramatic payoff for the 1.6-mile ascent. At 860 feet of elevation gain, this moderate route rewards those who persevere with sweeping views of the Salt River Valley below.

Trail No. II
Siphon Draw Challenges Experienced Hikers with 2,045-Foot Ascent
By Our Outdoor Correspondent · Lost Dutchman State Park

Named for the geological feature that channels seasonal rainwater from the Superstition Mountains, this strenuous route climbs 2,045 feet over 2.1 miles of increasingly technical terrain. The Flatiron formation at the summit offers unparalleled views of the entire East Valley.

Trail No. III
Peralta Trail Offers Iconic Weaver's Needle Vistas
By Our Outdoor Correspondent · Superstition Wilderness

The Peralta Trail winds through classic Sonoran Desert terrain — saguaro cacti, palo verde trees, and volcanic boulder fields — before presenting the hiker with a dramatic view of Weaver's Needle, the iconic spire that has defined the Superstitions for centuries of travelers.

Trail No. IV
Hieroglyphic Trail Draws History Enthusiasts to Ancient Rock Art
By Our Outdoor Correspondent · Superstition Wilderness

At the end of this accessible trail lies one of the most remarkable cultural artifacts in the East Valley: a collection of Hohokam petroglyphs, carved into dark basalt boulders more than 700 years ago. The route itself is suitable for beginners, making this trail a genuine hidden gem.

Trail No. V
Red Mountain's Volcanic Interior Astonishes All Who Enter
By Our Outdoor Correspondent · Red Mountain Recreation Area, Tonto National Forest

Perhaps no trail in the East Valley delivers such immediate visual drama as Red Mountain. Within yards of the trailhead, hikers enter a landscape of ochre and crimson — volcanic cinders and basalt columns that form a natural amphitheater inside an ancient cinder cone. Children and seniors alike have reported this as among the most memorable outdoor experiences of their lives.

The 400-foot elevation gain is modest by East Valley standards, but the experiential reward is outsized. Columns of red rock tower overhead as the trail winds into the interior of the mountain, creating a sense of discovery that belies the route's accessible difficulty rating.
